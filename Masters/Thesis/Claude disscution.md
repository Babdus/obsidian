Yes, exactly! Aktionsart (or lexical aspect) refers to the inherent aspectual properties of verbs as determined by their core meaning, regardless of how they're grammatically marked. This is distinct from grammatical aspect (like the perfective/imperfective distinction your morpheme seems to mark).

Verbs with different Aktionsart characteristics often interact differently with aspect-marking morphology. For instance:

- **Stative verbs** (know, love, believe) might resist perfectivization or show special semantic effects when combined with perfective markers
- **Achievement verbs** (reach, find, die) with their inherent endpoint might show different patterns than activities
- **Accomplishment verbs** (build, write, read) with duration and endpoint might interact differently
- **Activity verbs** (run, swim, talk) without inherent endpoints might show yet another pattern
- **Semelfactive verbs** (cough, knock, blink) denoting instantaneous events often have special interactions

When examining how your morpheme interacts with different verbs, considering Aktionsart might reveal systematic patterns. For example, perhaps your morpheme:

- Changes only aspect with accomplishment verbs
- Creates future meaning with activity verbs
- Is incompatible with certain stative verbs
- Creates conditional meanings primarily with achievement verbs
